The Weight of Force: Seattle Police Incidents Across Race and Time

Andy Kim & Ryan Kim

Credit: The Weight of Force: Seattle Police Incidents Across Race and Time by Andy Kim

Write-up

The question we were trying to answer with our visualization is: "Do the different levels of police use of force in Seattle fall disproportionately on certain racial groups, and how has that distribution shifted across 2014-2022?"

At first we thought of using a stacked area chart where the area would show how the sheer volume of the incidents involving each racial group during that year, but we also wanted to show the intensity of police force used, so we thought using two separated bar charts was more suitable when we decided to compare both the intensity of police force used in incidents separated by race and total incident magnitude also separated by race.

Thus, we decided to use one grouped and one regular bar chart where the left shows the increasing intensity of police force used for the incidents separated by race, and the right shows the total number of incidents by race. For the right chart, we decided not to use a color encoding since the racial groups were already labeled in the x-axis and categorizing the races by color would cause redundancy in information, since the nominal categorical data differs by nature. For the left chart, we decided to use a color encoding for this visualization since adding the three hue sequential scale would reinforce the severity of the ordinal data. We grouped the data by race where each group has three bars. Each bar correspondant to a level of force used by the police. Effectively showcasing the severity of the force used by the police towards each racial group in relation to all the incidents involving that race.

In both visualizations we made the LabelAngle in the x-axis to be -30 degrees rather than having to read the label completely sideways for legibility.

There are three interaction designs we incorporated into our visualization, scroll, play button, and tooltip on hover. Comparing the incidents and the police force used in the same year was necessary so we added a year slider so that the audience can switch back and forth among the years from 2014 to 2022 to see the differnce in total incidents and intensity of police force used.

The play button was added to show the transition from 2014 to 2022 more efficiently without having to scroll through each one. When looking at the charts from each year, it might seem like the range of the y-axis is not fitting for some of the visualizations, however, we made the decision to make the transition run a little smoother and allow the viewers to really see how the incident rates change and the intensity of the police force change throughout time. Making all the charts the follow the same y-scale allows the views to compare the different charts from different years more efficiently without having to pay attention to the axes if they were constantly changing as they were originally.

When hovering over the bars, the audience can view the different statistics on what the bars are representing. The left visualization tooltip hover displays the level-of-force used by the police in total incidents involving that racial group within the year, and a broader comparison to the total incidents for that level-of-force used across all races. For the right visualization, the tooltip hover displays the race category with the total number of incidents for the respective group along with a comaprison to all the incidents across every racial group within the same year.

During the planning phase, while determining what information we wanted to include in our tooltip hover, we compared different comparisons of data and what story we wanted to tell. Additionally, we considered what information was already more readily accessible by nature of the visualization encoding decisions we had already made. The grouped bars visualization allows the audience to compare the level-of-force used within each race to each other but it is not easy to compare the level-of-force used across all races since they are not grouped. Hence, we made the decision to showcase this comparison by including the percentages in the tooltip hover.

We decided to use Observable and embed it into the gitlab rather than using vega-lite and came to realize that we cannot work together on observable together at the same time. So, after we brainstormed ideas on how to present our data, we split the work for one person to do the coding on observable and the other person to do the report as well as getting the repository situated. This assignment took about 12 hours. We spent the most time (several hours) on the design decisions and the planning phase of the project. Thinking of the best way to represent our data, what fields we wanted to use, what story we wanted to tell, and how we would most effectively achieve this through the visualizations.
